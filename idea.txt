Module 1: AI Data Ingestion
The real problem isn't parsing files — it's that nobody uploads anything. Every team will build a drag-and-drop uploader. That's not the bottleneck; friction to start is. Students never build a "digital identity" because it requires manual effort they never get around to.
Unique twist — Zero-Effort Capture:
Instead of "upload your certificate," let the system pull data passively:

A WhatsApp/Telegram bot where you just forward a certificate PDF or internship offer email — it auto-ingests, no dashboard visit needed.
Connect GitHub — it doesn't just store the repo link, it reads README + commit history + languages used to extract real skill evidence automatically.
Email-forwarding address (you@myjourney.ai) — forward any acceptance/certificate email and it's captured.

This reframes the pitch from "storage app" to "the first digital identity system that builds itself." Massive demo wow-factor: forward one email live during your presentation and watch it categorize itself.
Module 2: Intelligent Categorization
The real problem isn't sorting into 6 buckets — it's that a label alone is a lie. "Python Certification" from a 2-hour Udemy course and "Python Certification" from a rigorous IIT course look identical in every competitor's system.
Unique twist — Trust & Depth Scoring:
Every item gets two AI-derived scores, not just a category:

Verifiability score — cross-check issuer domain, look for a QR/verification link, flag "unverifiable" vs "verified."
Depth score — infer proficiency (exposure / working knowledge / demonstrated mastery) from context, not the label. A cert = exposure. A cert + 2 related projects = working knowledge. A cert + project + internship using it = mastery.

This turns categorization from filing into credibility intelligence — genuinely useful and something judges haven't seen.
Module 3: Relationship Engine
The real problem isn't drawing edges between Skill→Project — it's that a static graph is dead the moment you build it. Everyone will submit a knowledge graph. It'll look impressive for 10 seconds and say nothing new.
Unique twist — Skill Growth & Decay Model:
Make the graph temporal:

Skill "strength" increases with reinforcing evidence over time, and decays if unused (a skill last touched 18 months ago is flagged as "rusty").
The engine detects narrative coherence — does your journey tell a consistent story (e.g., ML cert → ML project → ML internship) or is it scattered? Score it.
It surfaces gaps automatically: "You claim Data Science but have zero projects combining Python + SQL — that's a hole in your story."

This is the difference between a graph that describes your past and one that understands your trajectory.
Module 4: Digital Journey Timeline
The real problem isn't rendering dots on a line — it's that nobody reads a timeline. It's a nice-to-look-at feature, not a "wow."
Unique twist — Auto-Generated Narrative + Shareable Outputs:
Instead of just a visual list, the AI writes your story like a biographer and turns it into usable artifacts on demand:

One click → "Generate a resume bullet from this project"
One click → "Write a LinkedIn post about this internship"
Detects inflection points ("this is where you pivoted from web dev to ML") and narrates them

This makes the timeline produce value, not just display it — closing the loop from "here's your past" to "here's content you can use today."
Module 5: Smart Retrieval System
The real problem isn't search — search is a solved problem and every team will bolt on basic RAG. Judges explicitly listed RAG/embeddings as expected, so baseline retrieval won't differentiate you at all.
Unique twist — Advisory Retrieval (GraphRAG reasoning, not fetch):
Don't just answer "show me my certificates." Answer reasoning questions by combining vector search + the growth graph:

"Am I ready for a Data Science internship?" → it retrieves evidence, checks the gap model, and answers: "You have 2 relevant certs and 1 project, but no SQL evidence — here's what to add."
This is the killer demo line, directly answering the brief's own success metric but one level deeper than "I never search folders again" → "It tells me what to do next."
